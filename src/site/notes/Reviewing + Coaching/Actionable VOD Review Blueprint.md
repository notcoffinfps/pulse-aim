VOD reviewing is the bridge between your brain’s understanding and your body's execution. If you want to stop guessing and start growing, here is the structured approach you need to take. Being able to effectively review your own VOD is beneficial both to your progression and your critical thinking. This document will explore and explain the basics of VOD reviewing to help you get started in being self-sufficient.
### **<font color="#00b0f0">The "What, Why, How" Formula</font>**
The most important step in VOD reviewing is obviously, writing down your mistakes, finding the root cause, and figuring out a potential solution to your problems. When you see a mistake, don't just write "I missed." That's useless data. Use this framework to help you write down valuable and actionable data:

- **WHAT**: Note down what you did wrong or could've done better.
- **WHY**: Find a possible reason as to why that mistake happened.
- **HOW**: Come up with a possible solution that might fix your problem and how it's going to be integrated into your training session.

If you're worried about not coming up with the correct solution, it's totally normal, especially when first starting out. VOD reviewing yourself can sometimes feels like you're "guessing"  the solution to your problem, feeling unsure if whether the solution you noted down is what would actually help fix the problem.

One of the ways to get past the confusion of finding a solution you need to stop viewing your solution as a permanent answer and start viewing it as a hypothesis that you need to test. This especially useful when you're reviewing yourself since you're not directly getting an insight from someone with higher understanding/skill level. This is different compared to coaching other people who are below your current skill level, and still even then, the solution you come up with might not be the best.

Here are a few ways as to how you can structure the problem-solving part to give your solution more valuable, even when you're not 100% certain:
###### <mark style="background:#ff4d4f">1. Comparison to Top Percentile Players</mark>
An easy way to have an idea if you're solution is valuable is to compare your run to a top percentile run. This can be from a world record run, but I recommend to compare yourself to someone that is at maximum two ranks above your current rank as to not overwhelm yourself when comparing the VODs.

You can't effectively diagnose an error in your technique without knowing what good technique looks like. Say for example 1w4ts; compare your runs to someone who's a top percentile static player, let's say someone like zeonlo. Once you've watched their VOD, it will be easier to get a sense of what you're doing wrong. Maybe you're lines aren't straight to the target when flicking, and if so, the solution shouldn't just be "aim better". Start simple with a solution that has a focus on the fundamentals, in this case, you can use pokeball scenarios to help you eliminate "swooping" motions. Along the way, evaluate and build upon your solution, get into more intricate and into the more finer detail.
###### <mark style="background:#ff4d4f">2. Testing and Refining The Solution</mark>
Once you've written down a possible solution, start experimenting while evaluating it, try to further reflect on it and if it can be narrowed down even more for your specific use case. Aiming is an iterative process, meaning it requires constant refinement and optimization, Frustration during this phase is a biological requirement for your brain to update its internal map.

Let's say you've chosen a scenario that you think might help with your problem, you can try to exaggerate the error first. Let's use pokeball as an example again; in this case you will eventually come to the conclusion that pokeball will punish over-flicking a lot, this is how the scenario works in the fundamental level. Proceed to apply your potential solution while playing the scenario again, focusing on form and technique more than anything else, then see if it actually helps you. If you find that it helps you, even if only slightly helping you fix your problem, you're moving towards the right direction. Like previously mentioned, you would later refine the solution even more (if needed) to make it more direct towards your specific error.
###### <mark style="background:#ff4d4f">3. The Rules of Good Technique</mark>
It's helpful if you know what makes up a good or ideal technique, those rules are;

1. Only make as much movement as necessary.
	- Reduce the amount of distance your crosshair travels.
	- Eliminate excessive movements.
2. Reduce the total number of distinct movements.
	- Make your crosshair movement look seamless.
	- Reduce jagged or movements that are easily seen as separate motion.
3. Make the movements as quickly as realistically possible.
	- Execute the movement as fast as you can without sacrificing the previous two rules.

If you're solution aligns with these rules, it's probably the correct solution to better your technique. These rules have been the corner stone of good technique, it's made even more popular and widely known by Viscose and her video regarding good aiming technique.
### **<font color="#00b0f0">Ask Targeted Questions</font>**
When reviewing a VOD, don't just watch it aimlessly hoping you'll come to a conclusion, instead ask targeted questions to help start the process of fixing the problem. Simple questions are sufficient enough, the goal of this is to have a sort-of checklist for you to fall back on when attempting to diagnose yourself. A few of the questions are;

- Is my pacing consistent? Do I randomly slow down during my run?
- How is my tension management? Am I "locking out" mid run?
- Am I making rookie mistakes and forgetting my form and fundamentals?

These are questions to kick start your reviewing process and helps you provide a direction to where your review is going to go.
### **<font color="#00b0f0">Actionable Coaching Plan</font>**
Finally, after reviewing your VOD and starting your next session, work on **ONE THING AT A TIME**.
Avoid trying to fix everything all at once, this leads to cognitive overload which usually leads to forgetting our form and fundamental aiming technique, essentially making your training session useless and end up improving on nothing.

There are a few ways of going about this, either separate your training into blocks, or focusing solely on one aspect each day, both have their advantages and disadvantages.
###### <mark style="background:#ff4d4f">1. Training Block</mark>
With this method, you're breaking up your training session into different blocks, each one focusing on a single aspect. In the context of aim training, this is usually present in well built playlists and routines.

Structuring your playlist with this in mind is one of the easiest form of building a good training program with quality in mind. However, you do need to have an understanding on scenarios and their purpose if you aim to create your own custom playlist, an example of what the structure would look like would be like the following;

> [!info] STRUCTURED STATIC PLAYLIST EXAMPLE
> Block 1
> >Focuses on over-flicking by using scenarios like pokeball to punish it.
> 
> Block 2
> >Focuses on pathing by using scenarios with 4+ target count.
> 
> Block 3
> >Focuses on flick speed by using scenarios with larger target sizes and pressure scenarios.

Say for example our biggest problem is with over-flicking, you can dedicate more time or incorporate more scenarios in that training block. This can be useful to break boredom by having variation in your training but still have them be separate sections for you to fully dedicate your focus on each one of them.
###### <mark style="background:#ff4d4f">2. Scheduled Days</mark>
This method uses each day to focus on solely one aspect of your aim, you've probably have trained with this methodology before, it's present in the concept and structure of the popular VDIM playlists. In the previously mentioned VDIM playlists, each day is assigned an aim sub-category for you to focus on, in our case, we want to use this methodology more tailored to our specific needs.

The concept still remains the same, assign each day a specific target/goal to work towards, but instead of assigning them to a sub-category, assign them to aspects in your aim that needs work. For example;

> [!info] SCHEDULED DAYS EXAMPLE
> Monday
> >Focuses on fixing over-flicking by using scenarios that helps this or by actively trying to under-aim.
> 
> Tuesday
> >Focuses on improving your pathing by using scenarios that are either chainable or with a scenario that has more than 4 targets.
> 
> Wednesday
> >Focuses on flick speed and pacing by using larger sized target for the flick speed and pressure scenario to also help improve your pacing.

This can be useful if you prefer an approach where you only need to focus on fixing one aspect at a time and then later compile what you've learned by playing a benchmark scenario (most ideally at the end of the week or at the end of your scheduled training cycle). 
### **<font color="#00b0f0">Closing</font>**
By isolating aspects in your aim and/or using comparison, you can work towards removing the feeling of "guessing" in your VOD review process and hopefully work towards being more aware about aiming and the intricacy that's present in it. Remember that you aren't just looking out for moments where you miss your shots, you're looking for reasons why you miss the shot in the first place and how you can improve it. Raising your skill-floor can be achieved more effectively by VOD reviewing yourself and making yourself more familiar with what you lack in your average aiming performance, it's done by ironing out the finer details and errors you find in your VOD and by further solidifying aiming fundamentals.

If you're feeling lost or maybe even frustrated during this process, know that a certain amount of frustration is a biological requirement in order to update your "brain's internal map". If it still feels tough to spot your errors confidently, remember the "What, Why, How" process, fall back on it to help you start thinking more critically and kick start your improvement process.
